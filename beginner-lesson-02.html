01 / 09 Hook 00:00–00:25

The statistic nobody beats

90% of traders blow their account. That's not an exaggeration — that's the statistic. And I was one of them. Blew my first account. Blew my second one too. My third one lasted longer, but the result was the same.

Today I'm going to walk you through the three real reasons this happens — not from some book, from my own experience — and exactly how I fixed each one. If you're just starting out, this lesson alone could save you the eighteen months it took me to figure this out.

Why it matters This isn't a scare tactic — 90% is a widely cited figure across retail trading studies, and it holds up whether you're trading forex, crypto, or stocks. The traders who survive aren't the ones who found a secret indicator. They're the ones who fixed the three habits below.
02 / 09 My Story 00:25–00:55

Four years, three mistakes

I've been trading for four years. Things are stable now, but the first year and a half was brutal. I made every mistake in the book, blew accounts, got angry at the market — I genuinely thought trading was rigged against me.

Then I realized: the problem wasn't the market. The problem was me. And the moment I honestly looked at my own mistakes, everything changed. The three mistakes below are exactly what I found when I finally looked.

Why this matters for you You don't need 18 months of losses to learn this. That's the entire point of this lesson — you get the pattern recognition without paying for it in real money first.
03 / 09 Mistake #1 00:55–01:45

Moving the stop-loss

This was my biggest problem early on. I'd open a trade, set my stop, and if price moved against me, I'd think: "okay, just a little more room." I'd move the stop. Price kept dropping, I'd move it again — or just delete it entirely, telling myself it had to reverse eventually. It doesn't have to. And most of the time, it didn't. So instead of a small -1% loss, I'd end up down -10%, -20% — sometimes the whole account. Why do we do this? Because moving your stop means admitting you were wrong, and your brain hates that. It's easier to hope. The market doesn't care about your hope.

Common mistake Deleting the stop-loss entirely feels different from "just moving it once," but it's the same decision repeated with more confidence and less honesty. If you've ever removed a stop "just for this one trade," that's the exact moment this mistake takes hold.
04 / 09 Fix #1 01:45–02:15

The stop-loss is sacred

Here's the rule I put in place: the stop-loss is sacred. Once it's set, you never touch it. If a trade goes against you, that's fine — that's part of the game. You're not going to be right 100% of the time. Your only job is to limit the damage, and your stop does exactly that, as long as you leave it alone.

I started treating my stop like insurance: pay a small, known cost now, so you don't lose everything later. After that shift, my results changed — not because I got better at predicting direction, but because my losing trades stopped wiping out my account.

Try this Before you enter any trade, write your stop-loss price down somewhere outside your trading platform — a notes app, a notebook. If you find yourself wanting to move it, the friction of having it written down elsewhere makes the habit much easier to catch.
05 / 09 Mistake #2 02:15–03:00

No system

This was my second phase. I'd already stopped moving my stop, but I was entering trades with no real rules. I'd see something on the chart and jump in. Read some news and jump in. Someone in a chat said "buy" — I jumped in too. No system, no rules, just reacting to whatever was in front of me. What happened? Sometimes I made money, sometimes I lost — but overall, always in the red. Why doesn't this work? Because without a system, you have no idea what's actually working and what isn't. You can't improve what you can't measure. That's not trading. That's gambling.

Common mistake Notice this isn't about being wrong on direction — random entries can still occasionally win. The real cost is that you learn nothing from any of them, because there was no consistent rule to confirm or break.
06 / 09 Fix #2 03:00–03:30

Build a system

Here's how I fixed it: I built a simple system. I wrote the rules down on paper — when to enter, when not to. Where's my stop, where's my target, what timeframe, what pairs. Everything specific, everything written down. And the key part: I followed those rules every single time, not just when it was convenient. If a setup didn't match my rules, I didn't trade it, even if my gut told me price was about to move. A system removes emotion. It gives you repeatability, and it lets you actually see what's working and what isn't. Without a system, you're a gambler. With one, you're a trader.

Try this Your first system doesn't need to be sophisticated. Pick one setup you already half-recognize — a pullback to a trendline, a bounce off a support level — write down the exact entry, stop, and target rule for it, and trade only that for your next 20 trades.
07 / 09 Mistake #3 03:30–04:15

Overtrading

This became my trap right after I built my system. I figured: if the system works, trading more means making more money. Logical, right? Wrong. I started forcing setups that weren't really there. Saw something that almost looked like a setup — took it. Saw something similar — took that too. I'd sit at my screen all day hunting for opportunities. The result? A pile of junk trades. Money burned on commissions and spread, emotional burnout, and by the end of the month — a net loss. Good setups are rare, and that's completely normal. Quality beats quantity.

Overtrading — 10 forced trades
Quality — 1 clean setup
Common mistake "I have a system now, so I should use it constantly" is one of the most common traps right after Mistake #2 gets fixed. A good system tells you when NOT to trade at least as often as when to trade.
08 / 09 Fix #3 04:15–04:50

Trade less, trade better

Here's the fix: I capped myself at one or two trades a day, maximum, no matter what the market was doing. If there was no good setup that day, I didn't trade. I'd shut the computer off, go for a walk, do something else.

It was hard at first — it felt like I was missing out, like the market was moving without me. But when I actually looked at my stats: my win rate went up, my average profit per trade went up, and my overall results went up. Fewer trades, better results. Sounds backwards, but it's true. Patience is a skill, and it might be the single most important one in trading.

Try this Set a literal daily trade cap — write the number down, e.g. "max 2" — before the session starts, not during it. Deciding your limit in the heat of the moment doesn't work; your emotions will always find a reason for "just one more."
09 / 09 Recap

Three fixes, one foundation

So — three reasons 90% of traders blow their accounts, and the fix for each.

I made all three of these mistakes myself. All three cost me real money. But fixing them changed everything. Remember — trading isn't about being right. It's about not making dumb, avoidable mistakes. Protect your capital, follow your system, and be patient. Everything else follows from there.
