01 / 12 Hook 00:00–00:30

The battle you've been ignoring

Every candle on a chart tells a story. A story of a battle between buyers and sellers — who attacked, who defended, and who's in control right now. Most traders look at these candles and just see green or red rectangles. They have no idea what these candles are actually telling them.

Today, you're going to learn how to read that story. By the end of this lesson, you'll understand price action better than 90% of traders out there — and you'll never look at a "boring" candle the same way again.

Why it matters If you've ever stared at a chart with no idea what you're looking at, you're not alone. Most beginners are taught to spot "patterns" before they understand the basic alphabet those patterns are written in. This lesson is that alphabet — everything else in this course builds directly on it.
02 / 12 Anatomy of a Candle

Four price levels, one candle

Every candle has four key price levels: open, high, low, and close. The candle opens, moves up to its high, down to its low, and closes somewhere along the way. Those four points are your key zones, and every single candle on every chart you'll ever look at is built from just these four numbers.

The big rectangle — green or red — is the body. It's the distance between the open and the close, and it tells you how much conviction there was in that direction. The two lines sticking out top and bottom are wicks (sometimes called shadows). A wick shows the furthest price reached in that direction before pulling back. It touched that level, but couldn't hold it — which already tells you something about who lost the fight at that extreme.

Common mistake New traders only check whether a candle is green or red and stop there. But wick length and body size carry just as much information as color. A tiny green body with a huge lower wick tells a completely different story than a tall green body with almost no wick — even though both are technically "green candles."
4 numbers. That's it.
Open above Close = red
03 / 12 Bullish vs Bearish 00:30–01:20

Same shape, flipped meaning

With a bearish candle, it's the same idea, just flipped: the open is higher, and the close is lower — price opened up here and closed down here, which is why it's red, or "bearish." With a bullish candle, it's the opposite: open low, close high.

One more thing that trips people up early on — every candle represents a specific block of time, depending on your chart's timeframe. A candle on the 1-hour chart shows one full hour of trading. Switch to the 15-minute chart, and that same hour is now four separate candles, each telling its own smaller chapter of the same story.

Why it matters This is exactly why the same price move can look completely different depending on which timeframe you're viewing. A sharp "reversal" on the 5-minute chart might just be a tiny, meaningless wiggle on the 4-hour chart. Always know which timeframe you're reading before you react to what you see.
04 / 12 The Market Is a Battle

Not pictures. A record of a fight.

Candles aren't just pictures — they're a visual record of a fight between buyers and sellers. Buyers push, price goes up. Sellers push, price goes down. Think of it as a tug-of-war: whichever side pulls harder during that candle's window of time determines where price ends up, and the rope marks — the wicks — show you exactly how far each side managed to drag it before losing ground.

The market is a constant tug-of-war, and every single candle shows you who won that particular round. Zoom out far enough, and a whole trend is just a series of rounds one side kept winning.

05 / 12 Buying vs Selling Pressure 01:20–02:35

Color isn't everything

Look at a candle with a small body and a long wick on one side. Even if it closed red (bearish), a long lower wick tells you buyers pushed back hard before the close — there was real buying pressure at that low, even though the candle "lost." Flip it around, and the same logic applies to sellers rejecting a high. Key takeaway: candle color isn't everything — you have to look at the wicks. A candle can close red and still be a weak bearish candle, or close green and still be a weak bullish one.

06 / 12 The 3 Types of Candles

Forget the thousands of patterns.

There are hundreds of named candlestick patterns out there, and trying to memorize all of them is exactly how most beginners burn out. Here's the shortcut: nearly every one of those patterns is really just a variation of three underlying types. Learn to spot these three, and you'll read a chart like an open book — patterns and all.

07 / 12 Type 1 — Strength Candle

Big body, small wicks

Price opens and moves confidently to close near its extreme. Tiny wicks just show minor resistance that got overpowered along the way. A rare version has no wick at all — zero resistance on the opposing side, and a strong signal the next candle continues the same direction. Depending on context, a strength candle can also show up as an engulfing candle — one candle completely swallowing the price range of several previous candles. That's a full takeover in control, all happening in a single bar.

Why it matters An engulfing candle isn't just "a big candle" — it specifically needs to open past the prior candle's close and close past the prior candle's open, fully swallowing its body. That's what marks a genuine shift in control, not just a normal strong move in the trend's own direction.
08 / 12 Type 2 — Reversal Candle Control Shift

Small body, one long wick

Price pushes hard in one direction — just like a strength candle starting out — but the opposite side steps in, takes control, and drags price back. It closes way off its extreme. The initial move was real, but the other side ultimately won that round. Context matters a lot here. At a key support or resistance level, this is a strong reversal signal. If you've heard of a Pin Bar — that's this exact pattern wearing a different name.

Common mistake A reversal candle floating in the middle of a range, with no key level nearby, means far less than the exact same candle shape sitting right at a swing high or a well-tested support zone. Shape alone is never the full signal — location is what gives it weight.
09 / 12 Type 3 — Indecision Candle Doji

Nobody's in control yet

Structurally similar to a reversal candle, but the open and close sit almost at the same level. Buyers pushed up, sellers pushed down, buyers fought back — and it closed right around where it opened. Long wicks on both sides, small body. Rule of thumb: don't trade off this candle directly — wait for the next candle to confirm direction. If you've heard of a Doji — this is that pattern.

Why it matters A doji is a question mark, not an answer. It's telling you the tug-of-war ended in a draw — which is genuinely useful information — but it isn't telling you who wins the next round. That's what the following candle is for.
10 / 12 Momentum Reading candle size

Big candles, big moves

Big candles mean strong momentum — lots of participants, lots of activity. Small candles mean weak momentum. Compare candles side by side: if each one is getting smaller than the one before it, momentum is fading and the move may be running out of steam. If each one is getting bigger, momentum is building — a good sign you're catching a genuinely strong move rather than a random wiggle.

11 / 12 Two Key Rules Timeframes & Levels

Two habits that change everything

If you only look at a 5-minute chart, you might see a bullish setup and go long — but zoom out, and the overall trend might actually be bearish. The higher timeframe always takes priority. Compare the same instrument on two timeframes below: the 4-hour view gives you the real story, the 15-minute view is just where you time your entry once you already know the story.

4H — the real trend (bias)
15m — noisy, entry only
1

Check the higher timeframe first

It gives you the bigger picture before you drop down to find an entry on a lower timeframe. Skip this, and you'll keep fighting the real trend without realizing it.

2

Watch candles at key levels

A strong candle at a key level may signal a break. A reversal or indecision candle there often signals a turn. Watching key zones saves you from analyzing every candle on the chart.

12 / 12 Recap

You read the market like a book now.

Candles tell the story of a battle between buyers and sellers — who attacked, who defended, who won. Two rules to carry forward: always check the higher timeframe first, and always pay attention to candles forming at key levels.
